Unit activities

Linus Torvalds of course defends his OS (Linux) while Andrew S. Tanenbaum defends Minix, which is a different OS with these differences to Linux:

  • Very portable runs everywhere, but this came with performance issues
  • Supports multithreading which linux did not at the time
  • Minix was developed with educational purposes and was not free
  • Minix is a microkernel-based architecture

Tony Travis, from the Imperial College added to the dispute, and said that Minix segmented architecture of PC application versions was quite constraining. David Megginson from University of Toronto also says that the microkernel architecture is the way to go, but Minix needs improvements, and given that Linux is free, more functionalities and corrections should happen more often that these to Minix. This turned out to be true, and it was already mentioned by Peter MacDonald from University of Victoria.

The discussion concludes with experts defining Minix as a very powerful teaching tool, but not more than that, while Linux being the preferred solution for daily use. Minix lagged behind in implementations and support, slowly fading away from the picture.

Salah et al. (2016) explains that monolithic architectures are to be deployed in a single unit. Even small implementations and updates require the redeployment of the whole unit, despite big parts of it being unchanged. Maintainability is also a difficult solution as some applications are so big that replacing developers and implementing updates get increasingly complex, in some cases, even impossible. This is why applications are starting to be deployed over more scalable Service Oriented Architectures (SOAs) pay on the go hardware on the cloud solutions. These will require more monitoring and rely on strong network communication, hence these must be ensured and maintained. Migrating the architecture towards a microservices’ one comes at a huge cost as well. Fritzsch et al. (2019) takes on the challenge to find efficient ways to ensure a safe and smooth migration, for services to be integrated seamlessly. His research concluded that the best approach is “Service Cutter” but comes with the great limitation of finding extensive specifications of the system to adequately couple the units, and this is in many cases not available or easy to find.

To wrap up, given all the literature consulted I can conclude that I agree with this statement “Torvalds has been proven wrong and it only took nearly thirty years. Microservices and microkernels are the future.“ This is because users demand far more rich and interactive experiences which are supported by big application packages that demand a lot of resources. With the increasing complexity of applications monolithic architectures simply fall short due to the already mentioned constraints.
